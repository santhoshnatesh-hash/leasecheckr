Lease vs. Buy: When Does Leasing Actually Make Financial Sense?

10 min readUpdated March 2026
Key Takeaways
  • Leasing is cheaper per month but more expensive per mile of ownership over 10+ years
  • Lease if you want a new car every 3 years and drive under 15,000 miles/year
  • Buy if you plan to keep the car 5+ years or drive high mileage
  • NJ's monthly tax structure makes leasing relatively more attractive here
  • Your answer depends on how long you keep cars — not on the monthly payment

The lease vs. buy question has no universal answer. Anyone who tells you one is always better than the other is either selling you something or oversimplifying. The right choice depends on how you use cars, how long you keep them, and what you value financially.

This guide gives you a framework for making the decision based on your own situation — not dealer advice, not financial influencer hot takes.

The core trade-off

Leasing means you pay only for the depreciation you use, then give the car back. Lower monthly payment, but you're always making a payment. You never own the asset.

Buying means you pay for the entire car over time. Higher monthly payment, but once it's paid off, you own it outright. The car becomes a depreciating asset you can drive for years without payments.

The chart tells the story: leasing has lower payments for the first 3-5 years, but buying overtakes it once the loan is paid off. If you keep a car for 7+ years, buying almost always wins on total cost. If you get a new car every 3 years, leasing often costs less — especially in NJ with the favorable tax treatment.

Leasing makes sense when...

You want a new car every 2-4 years. If you enjoy having the latest technology, safety features, and warranty coverage, leasing lets you cycle through vehicles without the hassle of selling or trading in a depreciating asset.

You drive under 15,000 miles per year. Lease mileage penalties (typically $0.15-$0.30 per mile over) can add up fast. If you consistently drive over 15,000 miles annually, the math gets unfavorable quickly.

You want the lowest monthly payment. Lease payments are typically 30-40% lower than loan payments on the same vehicle because you're only financing the depreciation, not the full purchase price.

You use the car for business. Lease payments are often deductible as a business expense. Consult a tax professional, but the lease structure can be advantageous for self-employed NJ drivers and business owners.

Buying makes sense when...

You keep cars for 5+ years. Once a car loan is paid off (typically in 4-6 years), every month of driving is essentially "free" — just insurance, gas, and maintenance. This is where the real financial advantage of ownership kicks in.

You drive a lot. High-mileage drivers (20,000+ miles per year) face steep mileage penalties on leases. Buying eliminates this constraint entirely.

You want to build equity. A leased car is never yours. A purchased car, once paid off, can be sold or traded in for value. Over a decade, the ownership equity from a purchased vehicle can offset a meaningful portion of the next car's cost.

You want to customize or modify. Leases require you to return the vehicle in good condition. If you want to tint windows, add accessories, or make the car your own, ownership gives you that freedom.

The NJ factor

New Jersey's tax treatment of leases — taxing monthly payments rather than the full vehicle price — tips the scale slightly toward leasing compared to other states. On a $38,400 vehicle, NJ lease tax totals roughly $1,200 over 36 months vs. $2,465 if you bought the same vehicle. That $1,265 tax savings makes the lease math more competitive.

The honest answer

If you're asking "lease or buy?" the real question is: how long do you keep your cars? Under 4 years: lease. Over 6 years: buy. In between: it depends on your mileage, financial preferences, and how much you value always having a warranty.

Decision framework — answer honestly
How long do you typically keep a car? — Under 4 years favors leasing, over 6 favors buying
How many miles do you drive per year? — Over 15,000 makes leasing expensive
Is a lower monthly payment important? — Leasing is 30-40% lower per month
Do you use the car for business? — Lease payments may be deductible
Do you want to own the asset? — Buying builds equity, leasing doesn't
Is always having a warranty important? — Leasing keeps you in warranty permanently
