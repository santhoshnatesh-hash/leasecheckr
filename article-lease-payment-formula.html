How Car Lease Payments Are Calculated

8 min readUpdated March 2026
Key Takeaways
  • Every lease payment = depreciation charge + finance charge + tax
  • The cap cost (negotiated price) is the single biggest lever you control
  • The money factor is your hidden interest rate — and dealers can mark it up
  • Residual value is set by the manufacturer, not the dealer — you can't negotiate it
  • NJ taxes your monthly payment, not the full vehicle price — a real advantage

Every car lease payment in America is built from two charges: depreciation and financing. The depreciation charge covers how much value the car loses during your lease. The finance charge is the cost of borrowing that value — similar to interest, but calculated differently.

Understanding this formula gives you something most shoppers don't have: the ability to verify whether the numbers on your dealer's worksheet actually add up. You don't need a finance degree. You just need to know what you're looking at.

The formula

Monthly Payment = Depreciation + Finance Charge + Tax

Depreciation = (Adjusted Cap Cost − Residual Value) ÷ Term
Finance Charge = (Adjusted Cap Cost + Residual Value) × Money Factor
NJ Tax = (Depreciation + Finance Charge) × 6.625%

That's the entire formula. Every lease payment — Honda, BMW, Toyota, Tesla — is calculated this way. The differences are in the input numbers, and those are where the negotiation happens.

Adjusted cap cost — what you negotiate

The adjusted capitalized cost is the effective price of the vehicle for lease purposes. It starts with the negotiated selling price, then adds any rolled-in fees (dealer doc fee, acquisition fee, accessories) and subtracts reductions (your down payment, trade-in equity, rebates).

This is the single most important number to negotiate. Every dollar you reduce the cap cost lowers your payment for the entire term. On a 36-month lease, reducing the cap cost by $1,000 saves roughly $28 per month — that's $1,008 over the lease.

What to ask

"What is the selling price before any fees or add-ons?" — This separates the vehicle price from everything the dealer stacks on top. Negotiate the vehicle price first, then deal with fees separately.

Residual value — set by the manufacturer

The residual value is what the leasing company predicts the car will be worth when your lease ends. It's expressed as a percentage of MSRP — for example, 57% residual on a $38,400 MSRP means the end-of-lease value is $21,888.

The residual is set by the manufacturer's leasing arm (Honda Financial, Toyota Financial, BMW Financial) — not the dealer. It varies by vehicle, term length, and mileage allowance. A higher residual means less depreciation, which means a lower payment.

You can't negotiate the residual directly. But you can choose a term and mileage combination that gives you a better one. A 36-month/10,000-mile lease almost always has a better residual than a 48-month/15,000-mile lease on the same vehicle.

The money factor — your hidden interest rate

The money factor is your lease interest rate expressed as a small decimal — typically something like 0.00125 or 0.00189. To convert to an approximate APR, multiply by 2,400. So 0.00125 × 2,400 = 3.0% APR.

Unlike the residual, the money factor can be marked up by the dealer. The manufacturer sets a "buy rate" (the base money factor), and the dealer can add to it. This markup is pure dealer profit — and most shoppers never know it happened because the money factor doesn't appear on standard lease quotes.

Watch out

A money factor markup of just 0.0005 can cost you $500–$1,500 over a 36-month lease, depending on the vehicle's MSRP. This is the most common way dealers increase profit on a lease — and the hardest for shoppers to detect without asking directly.

NJ sales tax — the monthly advantage

New Jersey taxes your monthly lease payment at 6.625% — not the full vehicle price. This is a meaningful advantage over states that tax the entire capitalized cost upfront. On a $38,000 vehicle, you pay tax only on the monthly depreciation and finance charges, not on $38,000.

The tax is applied to each payment: (Depreciation + Finance Charge) × 6.625%. This means NJ drivers pay significantly less in total tax compared to states like Texas or Illinois that tax the full cap cost at signing.

Putting it together — a real example

2024 Honda CR-V EX-L AWD — Bergen County, NJ

MSRP: $38,400
Negotiated selling price: $37,200 (3.1% off MSRP)
Down payment: $1,200
Adjusted cap cost: $37,200 − $1,200 = $36,000
Residual: 57% × $38,400 = $21,888
Money factor: 0.00189
Term: 36 months

Depreciation: ($36,000 − $21,888) ÷ 36 = $392/mo
Finance charge: ($36,000 + $21,888) × 0.00189 = $109/mo
Pre-tax payment: $392 + $109 = $501/mo
NJ tax: $501 × 6.625% = $33/mo

Total monthly payment: $534/mo

If you see this same vehicle quoted at $412/mo with $2,500 due at signing, the effective monthly cost is actually $481 when you amortize the drive-off.
