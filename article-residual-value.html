Residual Value Explained: Why It Matters More Than the Sale Price

7 min readUpdated March 2026
Key Takeaways
  • The residual is the predicted end-of-lease value — expressed as a % of MSRP
  • It's set by the manufacturer's leasing arm, not the dealer — you can't negotiate it
  • A 5% higher residual saves more per month than a $1,000 discount on the sale price
  • Shorter terms and lower mileage give better residuals
  • The residual also determines your buyout price if you want to purchase at lease end

Most lease shoppers spend their energy negotiating the sale price. That's not wrong — but it's not where the biggest savings live either. The residual value has a larger impact on your monthly payment than any other single number in the lease formula, and most people never give it a second thought.

The reason is simple: you can't negotiate the residual. It's set by the manufacturer. But you can choose the combination of term, mileage, and vehicle that gives you the best one — and that choice can save you more than any amount of haggling over the sticker price.

What the residual means

The residual value is the leasing company's prediction of what your vehicle will be worth when the lease ends. It's expressed as a percentage of MSRP. For example, a 57% residual on a $38,400 MSRP vehicle means the leasing company expects it to be worth $21,888 in 36 months.

The residual determines how much depreciation you pay during the lease. Your depreciation charge — the largest part of your monthly payment — is the difference between the cap cost and the residual value, divided by the term. A higher residual means less depreciation, which means a lower payment.

Why 5% residual beats $1,000 off MSRP

Here's the math that surprises most shoppers. On a $38,400 vehicle with a 36-month lease:

Option A: $1,000 off the sale price
Savings: $1,000 ÷ 36 months = $28/month saved

Option B: 5% higher residual (57% → 62%)
Savings: ($38,400 × 5%) ÷ 36 = $53/month saved

Over 36 months: Option B saves $900 more than Option A

You can't negotiate the residual directly — but this is why choosing a vehicle with a strong residual matters more than getting the biggest discount on a vehicle with a weak one. A Honda CR-V with a 57% residual at full MSRP can be cheaper to lease than a competing SUV with a 48% residual and a $2,000 discount.

What affects the residual

Vehicle model: Vehicles that hold their value well (Toyota, Honda, Subaru, Lexus) typically have higher residuals than vehicles that depreciate quickly. SUVs and trucks generally have stronger residuals than sedans in the current market.

Lease term: Shorter leases get better residuals. A 24-month lease might have a 65% residual where a 48-month lease on the same vehicle might have 45%. The leasing company takes less risk on shorter terms.

Annual mileage: Lower mileage allowances get better residuals. A 10,000-mile lease will have a higher residual than a 15,000-mile lease because the vehicle will have fewer miles at lease end.

Trim level: Higher trims don't always get proportionally higher residuals. Sometimes the base model has the best residual percentage because its MSRP is lower but its resale value holds similarly to the higher trims.

Strategy

If you're flexible on term and mileage, ask the dealer for residuals on different combinations. The jump from 36 to 39 months might drop the residual 3-4%, costing you more monthly than the longer term saves. Sometimes the 36-month/10,000-mile combination is the sweet spot — lower payment than the 24-month lease and better residual than the 39-month.

The buyout angle

The residual value is also your purchase option price at lease end. If you leased a vehicle with a 57% residual ($21,888 on a $38,400 MSRP), you can buy the car at lease end for $21,888 plus any purchase option fee (typically $300-$500).

In markets where used car values are strong — as they have been in NJ — a high residual can actually work against you at buyout time because the purchase price is higher. Conversely, if a vehicle's market value exceeds the residual, you have instant equity. This is worth considering if you think you might want to keep the car.

Your residual value action items
Ask the dealer for the residual percentage — confirm it matches the term and mileage you want
Compare residuals across competing vehicles — a 5% residual difference matters more than a $1,000 price difference
Ask for residuals on different term/mileage combos — find the sweet spot before committing
Verify the residual is based on MSRP — not the selling price (it should always be % of MSRP)
Consider the buyout angle — if you might buy the car, a lower residual is actually better for you
