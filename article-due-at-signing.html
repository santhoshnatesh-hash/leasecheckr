Due at Signing: What Should and Shouldn't Be in Your Drive-Off

6 min readUpdated March 2026
Key Takeaways
  • Due at signing = first payment + acquisition fee + DMV fees + tax on first month
  • Putting more money down does NOT improve the deal — it just prepays future payments
  • A "cap cost reduction" lowers your payment but increases your risk if the car is totaled
  • Always ask for a line-by-line breakdown — this is where add-ons hide
  • Compare deals using "effective monthly" (total cost ÷ term), not the headline payment

The "due at signing" or "drive-off" amount is what you write a check for on the day you pick up the car. It can range from $0 to $5,000+ depending on how the lease is structured. And it's one of the most misunderstood numbers in the entire transaction.

The core confusion: putting more money down on a lease does not make the deal better. It just moves money from your monthly payment into an upfront check. The total cost of the lease stays the same — or in some cases, actually gets worse.

What should legitimately be in your drive-off

Standard due-at-signing breakdown:

First month's payment: $441
Acquisition fee: $595 (Honda) / $925 (BMW) / $795 (Toyota)
NJ DMV + registration: $200–$400
NJ tax on first payment: ~$29
Doc fee (if applicable): $0–$499

Typical legitimate drive-off: $1,265–$2,365

First month's payment

Standard practice. You pay the first month upfront, then start monthly payments 30 days later. This is non-negotiable and expected.

Acquisition fee

Charged by the leasing company (not the dealer) for originating the lease. It varies by manufacturer: Honda charges $595, BMW charges $925, Toyota charges $650. This fee is set by the leasing arm and is the same at every dealer. You can sometimes negotiate to have it rolled into the cap cost (which increases your payment slightly but reduces drive-off).

DMV and registration fees

NJ charges registration, title, and plate fees that typically total $200-$400 depending on the vehicle. These are government fees — non-negotiable and identical regardless of dealer.

Doc fee

NJ caps dealer documentation fees at $499. Some dealers charge the full $499, others charge less. This is negotiable — particularly if you're comparing two dealers and one has a lower doc fee.

What should NOT be in your drive-off

Watch out for these

Accessories you didn't request — floor mats, cargo nets, wheel locks, door edge guards. These are often pre-installed and added to the cap cost or drive-off without discussion. Ask: "Did you add any dealer-installed accessories?" and request removal of anything you didn't specifically want.

Protection packages. Paint protection, fabric protection, nitrogen tire fill, VIN etching, environmental packages. These are high-margin F&I products that often appear in the drive-off or cap cost. Decline them all.

Cap cost reductions disguised as "down payment." If the dealer asks for a $2,000–$3,000 down payment, that's a cap cost reduction. It lowers your monthly payment but doesn't change the deal's total cost. Worse, if the car is totaled in month 2, that $3,000 is gone — the insurance pays out the remaining lease balance, not your upfront cash.

Inflated fees. Any fee above the standard acquisition fee, doc fee, and DMV charges deserves scrutiny. "Dealer preparation," "market adjustment," or "delivery fee" are profit items, not legitimate costs. Push back on these.

The "effective monthly" trick

Dealers know that shoppers compare monthly payments. So they use drive-off amounts to manipulate the headline number:

Quote A: $389/mo with $3,000 due at signing
Effective monthly: ($389 × 36 + $3,000) ÷ 36 = $472/mo

Quote B: $425/mo with $1,200 due at signing
Effective monthly: ($425 × 36 + $1,200) ÷ 36 = $458/mo

Quote B is cheaper despite having a higher monthly payment.

Always calculate the effective monthly — total cost divided by term — to compare deals fairly. This is exactly what LeaseCheckr does when you enter your deal numbers.

The $0 drive-off approach

Some shoppers ask for "$0 due at signing" leases. This means rolling everything — first payment, acquisition fee, DMV fees — into the cap cost. Your monthly payment goes up, but you sign and drive with nothing out of pocket. It's a valid approach, especially for cash flow management, as long as you compare using the effective monthly cost.

Drive-off inspection checklist
Ask for a line-by-line breakdown — don't accept a single "due at signing" number
Verify the acquisition fee matches the manufacturer's standard — Honda $595, Toyota $650, BMW $925
Confirm DMV fees match NJ rates — $200-$400 is normal
Identify any cap cost reduction — understand this is a down payment, not a fee
Remove any accessories or protection packages you didn't request
Calculate your effective monthly — (payment × term + drive-off) ÷ term
Compare deals using effective monthly, not headline payment
