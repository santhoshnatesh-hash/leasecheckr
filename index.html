Why Shoppers Overpay

The monthly payment
is the least useful number in your lease.

$67
Avg. monthly overpayment observed in NJ submissions

Most shoppers negotiate by asking "can you get it under $400 a month?" Dealers are trained to work with that number — by adjusting the term, the mileage, or the drive-off in ways that look like a win but aren't.


Over a 36-month lease, a $67/month overpayment is $2,412 left on the table — often from a money factor markup that took a dealer 30 seconds to apply.

01
Monthly payment is a result, not a starting point
Your payment is calculated from cap cost, residual, money factor, and fees. Negotiating the payment alone leaves all the real levers in the dealer's hands.
02
Money factor markups are invisible in the payment
A dealer can mark up the money factor by 0.0005 and your payment barely moves — but you pay an extra $500+ over the term. Most shoppers never ask.
03
Due at signing can hide the real cost
Putting more money down lowers the payment but doesn't improve the deal. A lower monthly number with a high drive-off is not a better lease.
04
National averages don't reflect your county
A Monmouth County Honda CR-V deal looks different from a Bergen County one. Local verified data is the only relevant comparison.
The numbers that matter

Four numbers determine
your actual lease cost.

Your dealer knows all four. Most shoppers know one — the monthly payment. LeaseCheckr helps you understand all of them.

1
Cap Cost
Selling Price / Capitalized Cost
The negotiated selling price of the vehicle — the starting point for your lease calculation. Lower is better. This is the number most shoppers should negotiate first, before anything else.
Ask: "What is the selling price before any fees or add-ons?"
2
Money Factor
The Financing Rate
This is the interest rate built into your lease, expressed as a small decimal like 0.00189. Multiply by 2,400 to convert to an approximate APR. Dealers can and do mark this up from the base rate.
Ask: "What is the buy rate money factor on this vehicle?"
3
Residual Value
Predicted End-of-Lease Value
The percentage of MSRP the car is expected to be worth at lease end. Set by the manufacturer's leasing arm — not the dealer. A higher residual means lower payments. It's non-negotiable.
Ask: "What residual percentage are you using?"
4
Drive-Off
Due at Signing
The total amount due the day you drive off the lot. Includes first payment, acquisition fee, taxes, DMV fees, and any cap cost reduction. More down does not improve the deal — it just prepays future payments.
Ask: "Can you break down exactly what is in the drive-off?"

Four questions worth asking

What to ask
your dealer
before you sign.

Most dealers will answer these questions if you ask directly. The ones who won't are telling you something about the deal. Knowing the answers lets you check your quote properly — and negotiate from a position of understanding rather than uncertainty.

"What money factor are you using on this lease?"
The money factor is your financing rate. Dealers receive a buy rate from the manufacturer and are often permitted to mark it up. You have the right to know the number you're paying.
Affects financing cost
"What residual are you using, and is that based on MSRP?"
The residual should come from the manufacturer's leasing arm, not be set by the dealership. Confirming this tells you whether the payment calculation is legitimate.
Affects monthly payment
"What is the selling price before fees and cap cost reductions?"
The cap cost is what your lease is actually built on. A lower cap cost — achieved through negotiation or incentives — reduces every payment for the term of the lease.
Affects entire lease cost
"Can you itemize exactly what is in the due-at-signing amount?"
Drive-offs can include first payment, acquisition fee, DMV, taxes, and add-ons. Understanding the breakdown tells you if anything has been quietly added to the transaction.
Affects total cost
