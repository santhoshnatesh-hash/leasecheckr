What Is a Money Factor — and How Do Dealers Mark It Up?

8 min readUpdated March 2026
Key Takeaways
  • The money factor is your lease's interest rate — multiply by 2,400 to get the APR
  • Manufacturers set a "buy rate" — dealers can mark it up for profit
  • A markup of just 0.0005 costs $500–$1,500 over a typical 36-month lease
  • It doesn't appear on standard lease quotes — you have to ask for it
  • It's the single easiest number to negotiate if you know what to ask

If you've ever looked at a lease quote and wondered why the monthly payment seems higher than the math suggests, the answer is almost always the money factor. It's the financing cost built into every lease — and it's the number dealers are least likely to volunteer.

The money factor isn't mysterious once you understand it. But most shoppers never learn about it because dealerships have no obligation to disclose it upfront. That asymmetry is where the profit comes from.

What the money factor actually is

The money factor is your lease interest rate expressed as a small decimal. While a car loan quotes you an APR like 5.5%, a lease uses a number like 0.00229. They represent the same thing — the cost of financing — but in different formats.

To convert: Money Factor × 2,400 = Approximate APR

0.00125 × 2,400 = 3.0% APR
0.00189 × 2,400 = 4.54% APR
0.00250 × 2,400 = 6.0% APR

The money factor enters the lease payment formula as the finance charge: (Adjusted Cap Cost + Residual Value) × Money Factor. Because it's multiplied by two large numbers added together, even tiny changes to the money factor have a meaningful impact on your monthly payment.

Buy rate vs. dealer markup

Every vehicle has a base money factor set by the manufacturer's leasing arm — Honda Financial, Toyota Financial, BMW Financial, etc. This is called the buy rate. It's the lowest money factor available for that vehicle, term, and mileage combination.

Dealers are permitted to mark up the money factor above the buy rate. The difference between what you pay and the buy rate is pure dealer profit on financing. The dealer doesn't have to tell you they've done this. There's no line item on your lease worksheet that says "money factor markup."

How much does a markup actually cost?

Let's use a real NJ example. You're leasing a vehicle with a $38,400 MSRP and a 57% residual ($21,888). The finance charge calculation uses both numbers:

Finance charge = (Cap Cost + Residual) × Money Factor

At buy rate (0.00125): ($36,000 + $21,888) × 0.00125 = $72/mo
At marked-up rate (0.00189): ($36,000 + $21,888) × 0.00189 = $109/mo

Markup cost: $37/mo × 36 months = $1,332 over the lease

That $1,332 went directly to the dealer as financing profit. It took them about 30 seconds to add to the lease worksheet. Most shoppers never noticed because the money factor wasn't shown on the quote they received.

The hidden cost

On a $50,000+ luxury vehicle, a money factor markup of 0.0005 to 0.00075 can cost $2,000–$3,000 over a 36-month lease. The higher the vehicle price, the more the markup costs you — because the finance charge multiplies against both the cap cost and residual.

Why you probably don't know your money factor

Unlike a car loan where the APR is prominently displayed and legally required on lending documents, the money factor on a lease is not required to be disclosed on the deal sheet in most states. New Jersey has better disclosure requirements than many states, but even here, the money factor often appears buried in the fine print — if it appears at all.

This is by design. The money factor is the dealer's most profitable lever precisely because shoppers don't ask about it. If everyone negotiated the money factor the way they negotiate the sticker price, dealers would lose a significant profit center.

How to negotiate the money factor

The good news: the money factor is actually the easiest lease number to negotiate — if you know what to ask. Here's the approach that works at NJ dealerships:

Step 1 — Ask for the number

"Before we finalize, can you show me the money factor? I'd like to see it written on the deal sheet."
This signals you understand lease structure. Dealers give better terms to informed buyers. If they hesitate, that tells you the markup is significant.

Step 2 — Name the buy rate

"I know the base rate for this vehicle is around 0.00125. Is there any way to get closer to that?"
Naming the buy rate shows you've done research. Even if you're estimating, it puts you in a strong position. The dealer knows you know.

Step 3 — Use competition

"I have a similar offer from another dealer with a .00150 money factor. Can you match that?"
Competition is the most effective lever. Dealers don't want to lose a deal over the money factor — they'd rather reduce the markup and close the sale.
Pro tip

You don't need to get the buy rate to save money. Getting the money factor reduced from 0.00189 to 0.00150 saves $850+ over a 36-month lease on a $38,000 vehicle. Even partial improvement is significant. Don't let perfection be the enemy of savings.

How to find the buy rate

The manufacturer's buy rate changes monthly and varies by vehicle, term, and mileage. Here are the most reliable ways to find it:

Leasehackr Forum — the community regularly posts current buy rates from manufacturer bulletins. Search for your make and model.

Multiple dealer quotes — if you get quotes from 3+ dealers on the same vehicle, the lowest money factor you see is likely close to the buy rate. Dealers who aren't marking it up are competing on transparency.

LeaseCheckr reports — the full report includes money factor analysis, comparing your rate against what other NJ drivers are paying on the same vehicle. If your MF is significantly above the median, you're likely paying a markup.
